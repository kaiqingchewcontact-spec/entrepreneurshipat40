How I Built 7 Apps Without a Developer

A behind-the-scenes look at how one educator-turned-founder used no-code tools to build an entire product ecosystem.

It Started With Frustration

The honest origin of every product in the Beanstalk ecosystem is frustration. Not inspiration. Not a market analysis. Frustration — the specific, personal kind that comes from living inside a problem for years and finding that nothing on the market solves it well enough.

I was a teacher and a creator who wanted to build digital tools. I had ideas. I had domain knowledge. I understood what people needed. What I didn't have was a development team, a technical background, or the $50,000-$150,000 budget that building a professional web application used to require.

What changed everything: No-code tools matured to the point where a non-technical founder could build genuinely functional, beautiful products. Not prototypes. Not fake-it-till-you-make-it demos. Real products that real people pay for.

The First App — Learning by Shipping

Flowboard — a portfolio and link-in-bio builder with 3D flip cards — was the first. It came from my own need for a portfolio that felt as distinctive as the work it contained. Not a generic template. A genuinely designed space that matched how I thought about creative presentation.

Building it taught me more about product development than any course I had taken. I learned how users interact with things you've built — which is almost always differently from how you expect. I learned how to balance design ambition with technical reality. I learned that shipping something imperfect and improving it in public is infinitely better than waiting for perfection.

The No-Code Stack That Made It Possible

The tools I used to build seven products are available to anyone. Webflow for design and CMS. Beanstalk for app functionality and email list management. Zapier for connecting tools without code. Stripe for payments. Formspree for forms. None of these require programming knowledge. All of them are powerful enough for professional, revenue-generating products.

The key insight is this: you don't need to build everything from scratch. You need to understand which tools handle which problems, and how to connect them elegantly. This is a design and product problem — not an engineering one. Read our guide on how no-code changes everything for late starters.

What Each Product Taught Me

Speedeco, the AI presentation builder, taught me about the importance of speed as a core value proposition. Bean AI, the marketing strategy tool, taught me about the tension between automation and authenticity. Papaya Press, the indie publishing platform, taught me about community as a product feature. Each build made the next one faster, better, and more attuned to what users actually need.

Seven Apps Later

Seven products. No developer. No external funding. No technical background. Just a clear understanding of what problems needed solving, the right tools, and the willingness to ship before ready and learn in public. If you're thinking about building your first product, your first 90 days covers exactly where to start.
